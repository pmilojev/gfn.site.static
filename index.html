What this is

Not therapy.
Not wellness theatre.

This is training. It works on three fronts at once: psychological profiling, direct mindfulness practice, and applied behavioural work — what you actually do, say, and think in real life.

Profiling maps the recurring structure: how you tend to think, feel, defend, pursue, avoid, collapse, perform, overreach, withdraw, or pretend not to care. It shows where your personality produces specific patterns of suffering and avoidance — and where the same traits, met differently, become genuine strengths.

Mindfulness develops the practical capacity to observe experience clearly while it is happening — to meet difficulty without automatically obeying it.

And then there is the part most approaches leave out: applying that capacity to how you actually act, speak, and decide — in real life, with real stakes. The aim is not to feel better. The aim is to become less easy to overwhelm, and more capable of honest action under pressure.

On the name: "good for nothing" is not self-deprecation. It is a position. The work is not good for any specific outcome in the transactional sense — not for happiness, not for success, not for the elimination of difficulty. It develops a capacity that is then available regardless of circumstances. Which turns out to be good for everything.

Why the usual approaches stall

Your attempts to fix it are part of what's keeping it alive.

Most anxiety, panic, and stuck patterns are not made of a bad experience. They are made of attention that has gotten stuck on an experience that, on its own, would have passed. Two hard things felt clearly, one after another, are two hard things. Two hard things fused together, each pulling at the other, are not twice as bad. They multiply.

This is what anxiety, panic, phobia, and most compulsive and addictive patterns actually are: an experience that has never been fully met, because every time it shows up, something in you moves to manage it, explain it, avoid it, or shut it down. Your attempts to fix it are a part of it. The thing you're dealing with is, in no small part, made of your attempts to fix it.

This is usually why conventional approaches plateau. Therapy, medication, and most coaching work around the experience — with its history, its meaning, its management. That is genuine and often necessary work. But the felt thing itself, underneath the explanation, often goes untouched.

The work here, in plain terms, is to stop fixing and stop defending — on purpose, systematically — and to meet the experience instead. Attended to fully, as it actually is, it can finally be fully had. And what has been fully had stops needing to be fought.

About

Dr. Petar Milojev

The question that has organised most of my working life is a simple one: what makes a person and what does that mean for the quality of our lives?

I pursued it first through academic psychology — personality research, psychometrics, and large-scale behavioural studies across multiple institutions and countries. I have an actual PhD in personality assessment and over a decade of applied work using personality profiling and development in organisational and individual contexts. That work gives you a rigorous account of the person from the outside: stable traits, characteristic patterns, statistically predictable tendencies.

It turned out to be necessary but not sufficient. The outside view tells you where the grooves are. It doesn't tell you how to work with them.

That took a different kind of investigation — about a decade of serious study and practice in mindfulness and contemplative traditions, working with direct experience rather than statistical models of it.

The Good For Nothing Project is where those paths meet: profiling gives the map, mindfulness gives the path, and the day-to-day work of acting, speaking, and deciding is where the two actually get tested.

I am not a therapist and this is not therapy. If that is what you need, I will say so clearly and point you toward it.

Profiling

A map of the lens.

Psychological profiling maps how you tend to think, feel, defend, and behave — not as a diagnosis, but as a working hypothesis for where the real friction is and where the real leverage is.

What it tends to show is that the most fixed parts of how you operate — the traits you'd call your character, even the ones you're proud of — are usually organised defensively. Built to protect something. Which is also, often, where they cost the most.

The value is rarely in one dimension. It is in the arrangement: where strengths become traps, where vulnerabilities cluster, and where life keeps producing the same lesson with worse handwriting. The profile is translated into plain language and connected directly to practice — what to train, and where.

This isn't a one-time test. It keeps developing through the work itself — what you actually do, avoid, and prioritise tells us more than any questionnaire, and it shapes which practices and which work come next.

Coaching

Skillful discomfort.
In real life.

Coaching is built around direct practice, weekly work, and the live material of your life. Not motivational performance. Not endless insight collection. Not beautifully phrased avoidance.

We train attention across internal experience — thoughts, emotions, images, urges — and external experience — body, sound, sight, action, relationship, pressure. The aim is not to become calm. The aim is to become available to reality while it is inconvenient.

And then the practice has to work in real life. How you decide. What you actually say in the conversation you've been avoiding. What you do in the half-second before the old pattern takes over. This is where most approaches stop and where this one keeps going.

Who it is for

Sincere people.
Not necessarily serious ones.

Most people arrive here having already tried something else — therapy, medication, other coaching — and found that whatever shifted, the thing itself didn't fully move. This is for:
Anxiety — general, social, specific Phobias & fears Panic Compulsive & addictive patterns Stress & pressure Low mood & depressiveness Trauma loops & intrusive patterns Chronic dissatisfaction
Others arrive functional and capable, looking to build something most people never train deliberately:
Performance & performance anxiety Decision-making under pressure Self-trust & confidence Boldness & courage Clarity of thinking Relational difficulty Parenting & fatherhood
People who are aware enough to know something is wrong and honest enough to admit that information alone isn't fixing it.
High-functioning people who perform well in the world and are quietly miserable inside it.
People who have tried the standard approaches and found them insufficient — not because those approaches are bad, but because the problem is not what they thought it was.
Parents, professionals, athletes, and builders of difficult lives who want a private, direct, unsentimental space to work honestly.
