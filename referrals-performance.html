For coaches & development practitioners

An edge most training never gets to.

Information for life coaches, L&D practitioners, and sports and performance coaches considering The Good For Nothing Project for a client or athlete whose performance gap under pressure isn't about skill or strategy.

Background

What this is

Beyond resilience.

Most performance and resilience training is organised around recovery — bouncing back faster, managing stress better, tolerating more. This work goes a step earlier: training the attentional capacity that means quality of decision-making, presence, and execution doesn't degrade under pressure in the first place.

The core skill is what we call Being Uncomfortable Skilfully — meeting high stakes, uncertainty, and genuine pressure without the automatic defensive reactivity that narrows options and produces responses that weren't actually chosen. It's built from three trainable capacities: concentration (holding attention on what matters without losing it), clarity (seeing a situation as it actually is rather than through the distortion of stress), and equanimity (staying in full contact with difficulty rather than bracing against it).

This sits alongside performance coaching, L&D programmes, and sports coaching rather than in competition with them. It targets the layer underneath skill and strategy — the attentional capacity that determines whether skill and strategy are actually accessible when it counts.

Good fit for

Capable people, under real pressure.

Athletes — competitive performance & composure Leaders & executives — decision-making under pressure High-stakes professionals Performance anxiety Self-trust & confidence Relational & team performance Boldness & courage Clarity of thinking under load

This is for closing a specific gap: between how someone performs when conditions are easy and how they perform when it actually counts. There's no presumption of difficulty or dysfunction behind it — the attentional capacity that holds up under real pressure is simply not something most training ever addresses directly, deliberately, or at all.

Where a client's presenting need turns out to be primarily clinical — anxiety or a pattern that's significantly interfering with daily functioning, not just performance — that's flagged directly and a different referral (often back through clinical channels) is the right next step.
Working together

Complementary, not competing.

This isn't goal-setting, strategy, or skills coaching in your domain — it's the attentional layer underneath all of those. Most clients keep their existing coaching relationship running in parallel; the two tend to reinforce each other rather than compete; your work on strategy, technique, and goals, this work on the capacity to execute it under real pressure.

If you have a client or athlete who is technically and strategically sound but loses quality exactly when stakes are highest, that's usually the clearest signal this is worth a conversation.
